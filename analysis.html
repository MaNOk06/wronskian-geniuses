The four kinds of motion, side by side

We keep the weight and the spring the same and change only the resistance. Each curve shows how far the weight sits from its resting position as time passes, starting from a downward pull.

No resistance

A steady bounce that never dies down. The weight keeps the same swing forever.

Light resistance

Still bounces, but each swing shrinks until the weight settles.

Just enough

The fastest calm. Back to rest in the shortest time, no bouncing.

Heavy resistance

A slow, sluggish return that takes its time getting back.

The motion map

There is another way to look at the same motion. Instead of plotting position against time, we plot the weight's position against how fast it is moving. This turns the whole story into a single shape.

With no resistance the shape is a closed loop, going round and round forever, because no energy is lost. Add resistance and the loop spirals inward toward the centre, winding down as the motion fades. Heavy resistance does not loop at all: it simply sweeps in to the middle and stops.

Two things we double checked

Part of the project was reading the original study closely. Two small points stood out. Neither changes the results, but flagging them shows we worked through the whole thing rather than taking it on trust.

A name that does not quite fit

The original code runs the calculation through a routine borrowed from a different, more complicated kind of oscillator (the Van der Pol oscillator), whose resistance changes as it swings. Our system is the simpler kind, where the three quantities stay fixed. The answer it produces is still correct. The name is just left over from elsewhere.

A formula worth getting exactly right

For the sweet-spot case, the correct description of the motion includes a term that grows briefly with time before fading, written \( (1 + 4t)\,e^{-4t} \). A version that leaves out the growing part loses the very feature that makes the sweet spot special. Our graphs use the full, correct form.

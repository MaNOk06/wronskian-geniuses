One spring, one mass,
four ways to come to rest.

Hang a weight on a spring, pull it down, and let go. It bounces, then slowly settles. How fast and how smoothly it settles comes down to one thing: how much something is slowing the motion. This project works out the maths behind that, and lets you play with it.

What is this all about?

A weight on a spring is one of the most common moving parts in the world. On its own it would bounce forever. Add something that resists the movement, like a shock absorber, and the bouncing fades away. The big question we explore is simple: how much resistance gives the best result?

Too little resistance

The weight keeps bouncing up and down for a long time before it finally calms down. Think of an old car with worn out shock absorbers.

Just the right amount

The weight returns to rest as quickly as possible without bouncing at all. This is the sweet spot engineers usually aim for.

Too much resistance

The weight crawls back to rest slowly, like a heavy door on a strong closer that takes its time to shut.

In your car

Shock absorbers stop your car bouncing after every bump in the road.

On a door

The closer on an office door eases it shut instead of letting it slam.

In tall buildings

Skyscrapers hide huge weights that steady them against wind and earthquakes.

The maths behind the motion

Starting from a basic law of physics (force equals mass times acceleration), we build a single equation that captures the whole behaviour: the spring pulling the weight back, the resistance slowing it down, and the weight's own inertia. From there we can predict exactly how the motion plays out.

No heavy background needed. We walk through every step in plain terms, with the diagrams to match.

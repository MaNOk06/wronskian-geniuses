The maths behind the motion

How a spring, a weight and a shock absorber combine into one neat equation, and what that equation tells us before we plot a single graph.

The three parts

Picture a weight hanging from a spring, with a shock absorber attached alongside it. Pull the weight down a little and release it, and it moves up and down about its resting position. Three things shape that movement.

The spring always pulls the weight back toward the middle. The shock absorber pushes back against whichever way the weight is moving, draining energy out of the motion. And the weight itself resists changes to its speed. Balancing these three is all the model really does.

We assume the spring's own weight is tiny, the motion is straight up and down, and the three quantities below stay fixed while the motion happens.

The three quantities we control

Mass

How heavy the weight is. A heavier weight is harder to get moving and harder to stop. We write it as m.

Stiffness

How strong the spring is. A stiffer spring snaps the weight back to the middle faster. We write it as k.

Resistance

How hard the shock absorber fights the motion. More resistance means the bouncing fades sooner. We write it as c.

How we get the equation

The starting point is one of the oldest rules in physics: the push on an object equals its mass times how fast its speed is changing.

\( \text{force} = m \times \text{acceleration} \)

Two forces act on the weight once it is moving. The spring force pulls it back toward the middle, getting stronger the further it is pulled. The resistance force opposes its motion, getting stronger the faster it moves.

\( \text{spring force} = -k\,y \qquad \text{resistance} = -c\,\dot{y} \)

Here \(y\) is how far the weight sits from its resting position, and \(\dot{y}\) is how fast it is moving.

Putting the forces together and tidying up gives the single equation that runs through the whole site.

\( m\,\ddot{y} + c\,\dot{y} + k\,y = 0 \)

In words: the weight's inertia, plus the resistance, plus the spring pull, always balance out to zero. Give the weight a starting pull and let go, and this equation predicts every wobble that follows.

To find out what kind of motion it produces, we look at a smaller helper equation built from the same three quantities. The answer it gives splits the motion into four clear types.

\( \lambda^2 + \dfrac{c}{m}\lambda + \dfrac{k}{m} = 0 \)

The four kinds of motion

Keep the weight and spring the same, and change only the resistance. As you turn it up, the motion passes through four distinct kinds, set by whether the resistance is below, at, or above one special value.

No resistance

With nothing to slow it, the weight bounces up and down forever at a steady rhythm.

Light resistance

The weight still bounces, but each swing is a little smaller than the last until it fades away.

Just enough

The sweet spot. The weight returns to rest in the shortest time with no bouncing at all.

Heavy resistance

So much resistance that the weight oozes back to the middle slowly, without ever overshooting.

Problem definition

The mass, spring and damper system is one of the most important models in engineering and physics, used to analyse how objects respond to forces. It lets engineers understand oscillations, so they can predict how structures will move, absorb energy, and respond to outside events. Understanding how things behave when they vibrate is what makes it possible to design safer vehicle suspensions, rotating machinery, and sensitive instruments.

The ideas behind this model are used right across engineering to deal with vibration, from designing skyscrapers that can stand up to strong wind, to building robots that move more steadily. The same model also helps study the response of many other moving systems.

The system has three main parts: a spring, a damper, and a mass that hangs from the spring and damper together. The spring has a stiffness \(k\) and provides a restoring force. The mass \(m\) brings an inertial force, the resistance to motion that comes from mass and acceleration. The damper, with damping coefficient \(c\), changes the natural oscillation, and how much damping there is compared with the mass and stiffness decides how the system returns to its resting position.

The assumptions we make

  1. The mass of the spring is negligible compared with the body.
  2. Motion is strictly vertical, with no horizontal or rotational movement.
  3. The values \(m\), \(c\) and \(k\) stay constant and do not change with time.
  4. Movement downward is taken as positive, and movement upward as negative.

Building the equation

Picture a spring hanging from a fixed support. A body of mass \(m\) is attached to the bottom. Pull the body down a small distance and release it, and it starts to oscillate up and down. We measure the displacement \(y\) as a function of time. The motion follows Newton's second law, which says the acceleration of an object depends on its mass and the total force acting on it.

\( \sum F = m\,a = m\,\ddot{y} \)

Two more forces act on the body. The damping force opposes the motion, slowly draining energy and bringing the oscillation to a stop.

\( F_{\text{damping}} = -c\,\dot{y} \)

The spring starts unstretched. Once the body is attached, gravity \(mg\) stretches it by an amount \(\delta\) until the spring force balances gravity, and the body sits at rest. This resting point is the equilibrium position, and we measure \(y\) from there.

\( k\,\delta = m g \)

If the body is pushed down from equilibrium, the spring stretches further and pulls it back. By Hooke's law this restoring force is proportional to how far it has moved.

\( F_{\text{spring}} = -k\,y \)

Adding all the forces together with Newton's second law gives the final equation of motion for the damped mass and spring system.

\( m\,\ddot{y} + c\,\dot{y} + k\,y = 0 \)

where \(m\) is the mass, \(c\) is the damping coefficient and \(k\) is the spring constant. The starting conditions are the initial pull and the initial speed.

\( y(0) = y_0, \qquad \dot{y}(0) = v_0 \)

Where this shows up

Vehicle suspension

Keeps a car steady and comfortable over bumps in the road.

Skyscrapers

Helps tall buildings resist strong wind and other dynamic forces.

Rotating machinery

Controls vibration in engines, motors and spinning parts.

Robotics

Gives robots steadier, more controlled movement.

The maths behind the motion

Starting from a basic law of physics (force equals mass times acceleration), we build a single equation that captures the whole behaviour: the spring pulling the weight back, the resistance slowing it down, and the weight's own inertia. From there we can predict exactly how the motion plays out.
